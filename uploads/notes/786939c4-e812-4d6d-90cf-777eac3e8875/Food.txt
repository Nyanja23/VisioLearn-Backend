1. WHAT IS FOOD?
----------------
Food is anything we eat or drink that gives us energy, helps us grow,
and keeps us healthy.

In Uganda, we have many types of food:
- Matooke (steamed green bananas)
- Posho (maize meal / ugali)
- Rice
- Beans, groundnuts (peanuts), cowpeas
- Sweet potatoes, cassava, yams (in some areas like West Nile)
- Meat (beef, goat, chicken, mutton)
- Fish (Nile perch, tilapia, mukene/silver fish)
- Vegetables (dodo, nakati, sukuma wiki, cabbage, pumpkin leaves)
- Fruits (mangoes, oranges, pineapples, jackfruit, passion fruit)

2. WHY DO WE NEED FOOD?
-----------------------
We need food for three main reasons:

A. To provide ENERGY – for playing, running, reading, digging, and all activities.
B. To help us GROW – children need food to become strong adults.
C. To keep us HEALTHY – food protects us from sickness (diseases).

3. CLASSES OF FOOD (NUTRIENTS)
-------------------------------
Food contains different nutrients. Each nutrient does a different job in our body.

There are SIX classes of food:

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 1: CARBOHYDRATES (Energy-giving foods)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Give the body energy to move, think, and work.
- Examples in Uganda: Matooke, posho (maize meal), rice, bread, cassava, sweet potatoes, yams, millet porridge.
- If you eat little carbohydrates: You feel tired, weak, and unable to play.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 2: PROTEINS (Body-building foods)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Help the body grow, repair cuts and wounds, build muscles.
- Examples in Uganda: Beans, groundnuts (peanuts), peas, cowpeas, soybeans, meat, fish (especially mukene/silver fish for children), eggs, milk, chicken, grasshoppers (nsenene), white ants (nswa).
- If you eat little protein: Children become small (stunted growth), wounds heal slowly, hair and nails become weak.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 3: FATS AND OILS (Energy storage and warmth)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Give stored energy, keep the body warm, protect organs.
- Examples in Uganda: Cooking oil (palm oil, sunflower oil), shea butter (moo yaa), ghee, groundnut paste, avocado (ovacado), margarine.
- If you eat too much fat: You may become overweight (obesity).
- If you eat too little fat: You feel cold easily and have dry skin.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 4: VITAMINS (Protective foods)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Protect the body from sickness, help eyesight, keep skin and gums healthy.
- Examples in Uganda:
  - Vitamin A: Carrots, pumpkin, mangoes, pawpaw (papaya), green leafy vegetables (dodo, nakati).
  - Vitamin C: Oranges, lemons, guavas, passion fruit, fresh tomatoes, pineapples.
- If you lack vitamins: You fall sick often, get night blindness (poor vision in dim light), or get scurvy (bleeding gums).

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 5: MINERALS (Protective and building foods)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Build strong bones and teeth, help blood carry oxygen.
- Important minerals:
  - Calcium: For bones and teeth. Found in milk, small fish (mukene eaten with bones), beans, green vegetables.
  - Iron: For making blood (prevents anaemia). Found in meat, fish, beans, dark green vegetables, dried groundnuts.
  - Iodine: For brain and thyroid. Found in iodised salt.
- If you lack iron: You become pale, tired, and get anaemia (low blood).
- If you lack calcium: Bones become weak (rickets), teeth rot.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CLASS 6: WATER (Universal nutrient)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Job: Carry nutrients, remove waste, control body temperature, prevent constipation.
- Sources: Drinking clean water, soup, juicy fruits (watermelon, oranges), milk.
- If you drink little water: You get constipation (difficulty passing stool), headaches, kidney problems, and dehydration (dry mouth, tiredness).
- NOTE: Water is the only nutrient you cannot live without for more than a few days.

4. BALANCED DIET
----------------
A balanced diet is a meal that contains all six classes of food in the right amounts.

Example of a balanced Ugandan lunch:
- Matooke (Carbohydrates)
- Beans with groundnut paste (Protein + Fats/Oils)
- Dodo/greens (Vitamins + Minerals)
- A glass of clean water (Water)
- A piece of mango (Vitamins)

No single food contains all nutrients. You must eat different foods.

6. EFFECTS OF POOR NUTRITION (Malnutrition)
--------------------------------------------
Malnutrition means eating too little, too much, or the wrong types of food.

A. UNDER-NUTRITION (Not enough food):
   - Marasmus: Very thin, weak, no energy (lack of all foods).
   - Kwashiorkor: Swollen belly, thin legs, reddish hair (lack of protein).
   - Frequent sickness (malaria, coughs, diarrhoea).
   - Poor performance at school (cannot concentrate).

B. OVER-NUTRITION (Too much food, especially fats and sugars):
   - Obesity (very overweight).
   - Heart problems.
   - Joint pain.

C. DEFICIENCY DISEASES (Missing specific nutrients):
   - Night blindness (lack of Vitamin A).
   - Anaemia (lack of Iron).
   - Goitre (swollen neck – lack of Iodine).
   - Rickets (bowed legs – lack of Calcium and Vitamin D).

7. WATER – A SPECIAL NUTRIENT
------------------------------
We already listed water as Class 6, but it is so important that we learn more.

Sources of safe water in Uganda:
- Boreholes (protected wells)
- Tap water (National Water and Sewerage Corporation)
- Boiled water from lakes, rivers, or springs
- Rainwater harvested from clean roofs

How to make unsafe water safe to drink:
1. Boiling (best method – kill germs).
2. Using Water Guard or Purifier tablets.
3. Filtering through a clean cloth (if no other option).
4. Leaving in a clear plastic bottle in sunlight (SODIS method – for very clear water).

How much water should a P.4 pupil drink daily?
- At least 6 to 8 glasses (about 1.5 to 2 litres).

Signs of dehydration (not enough water):
- Dry mouth and lips
- Dark yellow urine
- Feeling dizzy or tired
- No tears when crying

8. GOOD EATING HABITS (Personal Hygiene and Nutrition)
-------------------------------------------------------
- Wash hands with soap and water before eating.
- Eat at least three meals a day: breakfast, lunch, and supper.
- Eat a variety of foods – not just posho and beans every day.
- Eat fruits (e.g., orange, mango) every day.
- Drink clean, boiled or treated water.
- Do not buy sweets, sodas, or fried snacks too often – they have empty calories (no nutrients).
- If you are a school-going child, carry a healthy lunchbox: e.g., boiled egg, chapati with groundnut, a piece of fruit.

9. ACTIVITIES FOR P.4 PUPILS (Do in your science notebook)
-----------------------------------------------------------
Activity 1: Draw a food plate and divide it into 4 parts.
- Colour each part to represent: Carbohydrates (brown), Proteins (green), Vitamins/Mineral foods (orange), and Fats/Oil (yellow). Label each.

Activity 2: Write down everything you ate yesterday (breakfast, lunch, supper, snacks).
- Next to each food, write the class of nutrient (Carbohydrate, Protein, etc.).
- Check if you ate a balanced diet. If not, suggest one missing food.

Activity 3: Match the nutrient to its function (draw lines):
- Carbohydrates   →  Protects from night blindness
- Proteins        →  Gives energy for playing
- Vitamin A       →  Builds muscles and repairs wounds
- Iron            →  Prevents anaemia (low blood)
- Water           →  Removes waste from the body

Activity 4: Group discussion – "Why do some children in our community get kwashiorkor?" (Possible answers: poverty, lack of protein foods like beans/groundnuts, not eating enough variety).

11. FUN FACTS ABOUT FOOD IN UGANDA
----------------------------------
- Did you know? Mukene (silver fish) is very good for children because you eat the whole fish – bones and all – which gives calcium for strong teeth and bones!
- Nsenene (grasshoppers) are not just tasty – they have three times more protein than beef!
- The Karamojong people in North-East Uganda traditionally drink fresh cow's blood mixed with milk – this gives them iron and protein during dry seasons when food is scarce.
- A ripe mango has more Vitamin C than an orange.
